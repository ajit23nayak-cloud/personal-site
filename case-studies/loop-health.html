Healthtech · Market Entry

Should Loop Health build a diabetes business for its 100k families? Yes — if the GTM is built on synergy, not on greenfield.

A focused market-entry exercise: does it make strategic sense for Loop — a health-assurance platform bundling group insurance with primary care — to add a Diabetes Reversal program?

SectorHealthtech · B2B2C FrameMarket attractiveness → ICP → GTM

The question

Loop today covers 100k families across 600 companies — expected to 3× this year. The call: should they start building a diabetes business for this population? If yes, how? If not, why not?

Frame: the Ansoff-style growth matrix

Existing productNew product
Existing TAM1. Market penetration2. Product development ← we are here
New TAM3. PMF expansion4. Category creation

Loop is at Q1 today. Adding diabetes to the same TAM is a Q2 move: new product, existing users. This is typically the highest-ROI next step before committing capital to a new TAM.

1. Is the market attractive?

  • India has ~100M diabetics and ~136M pre-diabetics (ICMR). Prevalence in urban India is 2× rural.
  • 75% of the India Inc. workforce is at higher risk of developing diabetes — i.e. Loop's exact core TG.
  • Applied to Loop's 300k-family forward base: ~2.25 lakh families are the addressable diabetes segment.

2. Who are we building for?

Personas

  • Employees / spouses under 45–50 with markers of pre-diabetes or T2D (high HbA1c, glucose, BMI).
  • Employees' parents with T2D — high-frequency monitoring + lifestyle.
  • Employees / spouses with gestational diabetes in 2nd–3rd trimester.

Pain points

For the end-consumer:

  • Diabetes is a pre-existing disease — 3–5 year waiting period in most group/personal policies. Meanwhile, out-of-pocket is steep.
  • High dependence on medication / insulin for working employees with poor lifestyle fit.
  • No preventive layer for the pre-diabetic — today it's "wait till you're diabetic, then claim."

For the HR buyer:

  • Elevated HbA1c drags productivity — fatigue, stress, secondary BP/BMI issues.
  • Chronic diabetes escalates claim frequency and pushes premiums up at renewal.

3. The go-to-market

What competitors are missing

Sugar.Fit, Fitterfly, Breathe Well Being all run 6–12 month diabetes-reversal programs. But they're B2C-led, with real customer-experience gaps: aggressive sales pushes, misaligned reversal incentives, thin diagnostic/hospital networks, and siloed treatment.

Loop's right to win

Don't build a standalone diabetes company. Build the diabetes layer on top of Health Assurance. Loop already owns the trust, the distribution, and the data. The unit economics change completely.
  • Core product: Unlimited 1:1 (virtual + in-person) with diabetologists and coaches. In-office visits by Loop diabetologists.
  • Incentive structure: Doctor/coach comp tied to HbA1c reduction, BMI drop, or medication reduction — not session volume.
  • Holistic monitoring: Blood glucose + markers in Loop's app; device-based monitoring as add-on.
  • Dedicated lanes: Gestational support, senior-glucose program.

Why this is capital-efficient

  • Reuses Loop's customer support, doctor network, advisor cadence, and app infra — low incremental capex.
  • Distribution is essentially free: 300k high-trust families already on-platform. CAC approaches zero at the customer level; HR-level CAC is a routine upsell.

Pilot & KPIs

Pick one org with high diabetes prevalence. MVP with a single diabetologist + two coaches + the app's monitoring module.

  • Input: # demos, demo conversion, # consultations, reversals / doctor / week, time-to-reversal.
  • Output: Paid users, reversals, MRR, NPS.

Three-month gate before org-wide rollout. If reversal rate + NPS clear bar, go. If not, kill fast — the synergy thesis is only real if outcomes compound.
